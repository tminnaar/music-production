Music Production

Travis Minnaar

Introduction

I've been producing music for nearly a decade, starting as a passing interest and eventually growing into an obsession for the last 5 years. I've been fortunate enough to have my music released on vinyl and played gigs in London and Bristol.

For the most part I produce dubstep, with my biggest influences being Headland, Causa and Digital Mystikz. I often dabble in Halftime Jungle too with Homemade Weapons and TORN being major influences. I'm drawn to genres with alot of space, rhythm and tension.

Tools

Hardware

  • Ableton Push: a midi controller to sequence melodies, beats and automation.
  • Mackie MR5 MKII Monitors: active two‑way monitors to reference audio with.
  • Focusrite Scarlett 2i2 Audio Interface: an audio interface with 2 inputs for recording external instruments.

Software

  • Ableton Live 11: my DAW (digital audio workstation) of choice, heavily focused on a frictionless workflow to flesh out tracks quickly while the ideas are fresh!
  • The Glue: an analog modeled compressor plug-in based on The classic 80's British big console buss compressor, great for controlling drums and 'glueing' them together.
  • Valhalla VintageVerb: inspired by the classic hardware digital reverbs of the 70s and 80s, VintageVerb has a wide array of modelled rooms and springs to choose from that make it really easy getting that nice old dusty reverb sound.
  • u-he Twangström: you can't make spacey dubstep without a spring reverb, and although nothing's as good as the real deal, Twangström comes very close.
  • xfer Serum: the bread and butter of the vast majority of electronic music producers, Serum is a wavetable synthesizer with frequency modulation capabilites.

Workflow

I find that the tracks I'm most happy with end up being the ones that are started and finished within the space of a few days. The longer it's drawn out, the harder it is to maintain the creative headspace you were in when you first started, so having a frictionless creative workflow is imperitive. Ableton opens in a template I have iterated over and over through the years, with my go-to channels for drums, synths, fx etc. already set up with my go-to plug-ins on. For instance, having 2 seperate reverb channels; a large hall and a plate reverb, allows me to send my instruments to reverbs with settings already dialled in for the space they require.

Seperating sound design and writing into their own sessions is another massive help, there's nothing worse than getting hung up trying to create the perfect bass sound for hours and squandering your creative headspace, making it harder to move forward with the musicality and arrangement of the track. On days when I'm not feeling too creative I try and spend my time synthesising bass sounds and creating interesting distortion, reverb and delay patches, much like a pianter would mix a pallete before starting a painting. Not only does this save you the hassle of getting too fiddly when you're trying to write and arrange tracks but also slowly nurtures your own signature sound over time that sets you apart from your peers.
